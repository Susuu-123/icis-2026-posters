Learning abstract relational terms in infancy based on cross-situational contrastive labeling

Lanlin Su, Elena Luchkina, & Elizabeth Spelke

The University of Texas at Dallas · Harvard University

About this study

This study asked whether 15-to-19-month-old infants can begin learning the meanings of abstract relational words such as same and different. Using a contrastive, cross-situational labeling procedure, infants saw pairs of images labeled "same" or "different," and were then tested on entirely new pairs. Infants who received the simplest, most structured input showed evidence of learning, suggesting that the foundations for abstract word learning may already be in place before the second year of life.

Why this matters

Children typically do not use words like same and different accurately until around age 2.5–3, so understanding whether infants can learn these words from clearer input helps explain why such learning is normally delayed — and how early conceptual and language development are linked.

About the lab

Dr. Elena Luchkina is an Assistant Professor of Psychology at The University of Texas at Dallas and directs the Early Cognition and Communication Lab (ECCL). Her research investigates the origins of human communication about things that are unseen, abstract, or otherwise not directly observable. The ECCL Lab combines developmental and cognitive science, using behavioral and eye-tracking methods, to study how early social interactions and communicative cues shape learning.
