How do 15-to-18-month-olds represent unseen objects when such objects are being named?

Elena Luchkina, Emily Yang, & Sandra Waxman

The University of Texas at Dallas · Northwestern University

About this study

This study asked how 15-to-18-month-old infants represent an object they cannot see when that object is named. Infants heard an experimenter name several familiar objects and then a novel, hidden object, while we varied the kind of input (labels alone vs. labels paired with visible examples) and how the category was framed. Infants reliably identified the named object at test, and — notably — labels alone were at least as effective as labels accompanied by visible examples, suggesting infants can build conceptual representations of unseen things from language.

Why this matters

A great deal of human learning happens through language about things we have never directly experienced, so understanding how early and how flexibly infants form such representations clarifies how language supports learning beyond the here-and-now.

About the lab

Dr. Elena Luchkina is an Assistant Professor of Psychology at The University of Texas at Dallas and directs the Early Cognition and Communication Lab (ECCL). Her research investigates the origins of human communication about things that are unseen, abstract, or otherwise not directly observable. The ECCL Lab combines developmental and cognitive science, using behavioral and eye-tracking methods, to study how early social interactions and communicative cues shape learning.
